# Dave.io - Pain Language & Messaging

*For Katie: this is how Dave talks about the problem it solves, validated live by Dave's CRO (Sean). Mirror the phrasings marked USE; avoid the ones marked AVOID. This is the voice that has already earned replies, so lean on it when writing openers, follow-ups, and objection responses.*

## The problem in buyers' words

- **"Nobody actually owns the infrastructure, so it keeps pulling the team off the product."**
- **"Handling deployments and putting out fires whenever something breaks."**
- Developers forced into infra work they are not expert at - "plumbing and firefighting" - which they hate, which kills their focus, and which breaks things because it is not their expertise.
- Larger teams: one or two DevOps **bottlenecking** 45-60 engineers, tickets piling up, bigger projects getting pushed.
- Hiring an infra person is slow (3-6 months), expensive (~$250K), and often needs recruiter fees.

## Phrasings - USE these (validated)

- **"Handling deployments"** and **"putting out fires whenever something breaks."** (Sean: "I believe in that.")
- **"Nobody actually owns the infrastructure."**
- The word **"bottleneck"** for stretched DevOps teams - universally understood by engineers. Personalize the ratio when known ("two DevOps covering ~50 engineers").
- **"Tech stack"** when describing what Dave connects to - not "code" (which sounds like scary source-code access). List the specific integrations (cloud, Git, ticketing, comms) to stay concrete.
- An **open-ended cloud question**: "Which cloud provider are you using today?" - not "AWS, GCP, or Azure?" (three options reads like fishing).

## Phrasings - AVOID these

- **Do not label teams "smaller" or "small."** It hands a 300-person / 50-engineer prospect an easy, irrelevant objection ("we're not small"). The real distinction is infra-owner or not, never size.
- **Do not say "keeping the cloud running"** - too niche. Infrastructure is a wide realm (CI/CD, Kafka, IaC, cloud resources); stay high-level enough to pull them into the conversation.

## The core wedge

Developers and infrastructure engineers are **two different skill sets and two different worlds**. Forcing developers to do infra work always breaks, short-term or long-term. When no one owns infrastructure, a malfunction becomes chaos because no one on the team has the expertise to resolve it fast. This wedge is the heart of the pitch.

## Tone

Curiosity-first, not a pitch. Open with a genuine question off their situation (the infra hire, who owns infra, how they handle it as they scale), earn the reply, and only introduce Dave once a pain is on the table. Non-needy throughout. A technical question back ("what are you using, Claude?") is a **buying signal**, not an objection - move toward a conversation, do not get defensive.

## Objection handling (from the validated call script)

- **"We handle infrastructure ourselves"** → agree it is common → ask if it is pulling engineers off the product → offer to show how Dave takes it off their plate.
- **"Not comfortable with AI in our infrastructure"** → agree the cloud is delicate → note Dave only **reads**, never writes, and a human approves every change → that control is exactly why it runs as a service.
- **"We could just use Claude / a copilot ourselves"** → agree they could → but that is not where the team's expertise is, and they would own all the cost and risk → that is the part Dave takes off them.
- **"No time"** → agree infra never sleeps → ask about priorities around freeing engineers or avoiding an expensive hire.
- **"Send info"** → ask whether to focus it on taking infra off their engineers or the cost-vs-hiring angle → send and follow up on what stood out.
