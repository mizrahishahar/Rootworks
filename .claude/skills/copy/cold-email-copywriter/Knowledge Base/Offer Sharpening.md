# Offer Sharpening

The offer reaches you as words: a number, a proof point, a guarantee, a deliverable. How those words land on a cold stranger is your job. You make whatever offer you are handed read as a no-brainer, using nothing but language, inside fixed borders.

The cold prospect is asking one question: is this worth my response? Your wording answers it.

---

## What you maneuver (full latitude)

- **Surface the number.** The quantified gap is the strongest thing in the email. Put it where it cannot be missed. "10 to 20 sales calls a month" beats "more leads" every time. If the offer has a number and the draft buries it, that is a miss.
- **Phrase the risk reversal in the strongest cold-facing words.** Same guarantee, sharper language. "55k in sales or you don't pay" and "20% more conversions, guaranteed" are the same risk reversal as "performance-based" but they hit harder. Specific beats generic: "8 booked calls in 60 days or a full refund" beats "results or your money back".
- **Lead with the proof that fits the segment.** When more than one case study exists, choose the one closest to the segment you are writing to. A roofing result sells roofers; a SaaS result sells SaaS. The right proof in the right email is a wording choice you own.
- **Compress to the no-brainer.** Cut every word that is not doing work until what remains is the offer at its most undeniable. Length is the enemy of a no-brainer.
- **Frame, do not fabricate.** Choosing which true thing to say first, and how to say it, is framing. That is your full latitude.

---

## What you never touch (the borders)

- **Never invent facts.** No case study, number, dollar amount, percentage, or client name that the brief did not give you. People's jobs depend on this. If the proof is not in the brief, the proof does not exist.
- **Never change the actual terms.** Scope, price, the guarantee's real conditions, the deliverable. You phrase them; you do not redesign them. "Pay per call" does not become "pay per lead" because it reads better.
- **Never promise what cannot be delivered.** A CTA that names an asset means the asset gets made. A number in the body means the number is real.

---

## When words cannot save it

Sometimes the offer handed to you is structurally weak: no real new result for the prospect, no proof and no risk reversal, a deliverable they do not value. No subject line saves that. Wordsmithing a broken offer just produces a polished email that does not convert and burns the list.

When you see it, say so. Name the specific gap to the operator rather than papering over it with language. Sharpening is for a sound offer that is being undersold. It is not a rescue for an offer that should not ship.

---

## The test

Read the finished email as the cold prospect. Is the number unmissable? Is the risk on you, not them? Is the proof the right proof? Is there a single word left to cut? If yes to the first three and no to the last, the offer is as sharp as words can make it. If the answer is "the offer itself is the problem", that is a flag, not a rewrite.
