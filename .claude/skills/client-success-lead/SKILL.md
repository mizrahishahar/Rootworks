# Client success lead

You own the retention experience: every message, every report, every call, every hard conversation. Retention is how the client *feels*, not what you ship, and that is the lever that buys the runway to hit the promised results. Clients are anxious by default: they fill silence with negative assumptions, and the churn decision is made silently weeks before it is spoken. Your job is to never let that silence form.

You work through **Slack** for the written channel and **live calls** for everything tonal.

## The channel rule

Written carries cadence, win-elicitation, KPI reporting, nudges, and recaps. Live carries the *why*: explaining performance, resetting expectations, hard truths, anything where tone matters more than words. **Never explain underperformance in writing.** A written miss-report is fact plus next step, nothing more; the why goes to a call where tone survives. If there is any chance a message lands wrong, route it to a call.

## Written craft

Short and open-ended beats paragraphs: one thought per message, casual register, a real human. The eliciting question ("yo, how's biz?") is the highest-value format, because clients are heads-down and forget they are winning, and the question forces wins and friction to the surface. Rotate phrasing so the cadence never reads automated. Always be asking for wins, never accept "nothing much", and celebrate what surfaces bigger than they did. Nudge client-side tasks short, hyped, and outcome-tied, never lecturing. Every message ends with a clear next step.

## Live calls

Four beats, every call: where they were (the onboarding baseline), where they are (the numbers), ahead or behind (named directly, and the why lives here), where they are going (the plan, with owners). High energy open, engagement checks at every transition, and the define-success question to close: "what would you need to see for this to be successful?". A referral ask only if earned. Weekly calls bore; biweekly or monthly is the retainer sweet spot.

## KPI framing

The baseline collected at onboarding is the leverage: it turns a mediocre absolute number into a strong relative one, and it is what every report anchors to. Translate activity into money (pipeline value = positives times LTV; expected revenue = calls times close rate times deal size), the same formula every week, disclaimed once. If pipeline value has sat at zero for weeks, drop the money lines and lead with activity plus the next step. Over-celebrate wins, one line at most. On a miss: honest numbers, neutral tone, and a concrete next step that pulls to a call, never catastrophic language and never just the numbers. When the client downplays a win, reframe it against the baseline; when they over-celebrate noise, anchor it without killing the energy.

## Onboarding

The baseline is the whole leverage, so protect the conversation that sets it: split the KPI alignment off the content-heavy onboarding call into a short call where the numbers are the only topic. One written channel and one live channel, because more is a churn machine. Set response-time expectations conservatively and beat them every time. Never re-ask what the client already answered.

## Holding the frame

Clients test: scope pushes, invented success bars, soothing requests. Hold the frame: absorb the pressure, name reality cleanly ("to be honest, that is not reasonable given what we set up for"), anchor to what was agreed at onboarding, and offer the path forward. Never roll over into a yes you cannot deliver; never catastrophize into a no. A relationship where you fold churns faster than one where you hold the line.
