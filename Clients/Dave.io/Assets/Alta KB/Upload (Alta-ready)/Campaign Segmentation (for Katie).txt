# Campaign Segmentation

*For Katie: segmentation is not a step done to a list, it is the campaign itself. How the audience is sliced decides what each campaign can truthfully say. A campaign that targets one specific kind of company, one persona, and one trigger can be specifically true in every message. A campaign that mixes them forces vague copy that speaks to no one. Respect the segment you are given, and write every message specifically true to it.*

## The one test

Every variable is one of two things, and the test is whether its value **changes the angle or just a word**:

- **Changes a word** - personalization. The city, the exact role, a detail about them. A merge field. It does not split anything.
- **Changes the angle** - a segment. A different value means a different audience, which means its own campaign with its own emphasis.

Both exist to make the copy specifically true for the reader. **Real segmentation out-pulls broad-list personalization.** Do not lean on clever per-lead personalization to paper over a mixed audience; the segment is what makes the message land.

## The axes we slice on

Read which of these actually separate the market into groups that need different copy. Not all apply every time.

- **Company metrics** - size and maturity, industry, the cloud and stack they run, funding stage. A small team with no infra owner and a large team with a bottlenecked DevOps function feel different pains and need a different angle, so they are different campaigns, not one campaign with a merge field. Size and stage change the language, so they change the campaign.
- **Trigger / signal** - the buying signal the audience is sourced on. A company hiring an infra engineer, a company that just raised, a company with a new engineering leader are three different reasons to reach out and three different openers. A different trigger is a different campaign. (This is also why a campaign carries one signal, not several.)
- **Persona** - the decision-maker being reached. A technical founder, a CTO, a VP Engineering, and a Head of Infrastructure care about different things and are opened differently. A different persona is a different angle, so a different campaign. Persona call-outs tend to land harder at larger companies; the company-level pain lands harder at smaller ones.

## Variants are not campaigns

Variants test different angles against **the same** audience, inside one campaign. Different copy to a **different** audience is a different campaign, never a variant. Reaching for "variants" when there are really two audiences quietly corrupts the read on what is working.

## As specific as possible, only where volume supports it

The drive is maximum specificity; the brake is volume. A slice must be big enough to be its own campaign and read a real signal. Fold a thin slice into a broader campaign rather than starve a standalone one. Think of it as a grid, company profile against persona against trigger: some cells stand alone, thin ones merge.

## What this means in practice

One campaign equals one company profile, one persona, one trigger. That is the unit that lets the copy be specifically true and lets the results actually tell you something. The segmentation is decided upstream, when the audience is built; the job here is to write each campaign so specifically to its slice that the segment does half the persuading before a word is chosen.
