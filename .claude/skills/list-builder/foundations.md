# Foundations

Thumb-rules, not laws; every number is directional. The one that governs all of them: **the list is the lever.** When a campaign is dead the list is the suspect roughly 80% of the time, not the copy. You do not get the list right by thinking harder up front; you get it right by sending and reading the data.

## Mindset

- The first list is a hypothesis; the ICP gets refined in the inbox, not the spreadsheet. Ship the campaign you have rather than chase certainty before a single send.
- Count contacts, not companies: a 1,000-company TAM can be 5,000 to 10,000 contacts, which alone flips "too small" into viable.
- Narrow enough means one specific copy line is true for everyone on the list. That is the test, not a TAM number.

## Sizing (the ratios that back-calc a list)

- ~1 meeting per 5,000 sends, ~1 positive per 750, ~1% reply, bounce under ~3%. Judge nothing under ~5,000 sends; trust an angle at 10 to 15 positives.
- Build ~1,500 to 2,500 leads per test cohort, ~1,000 to 2,000 per native segment, and hold ~10,000 to 15,000 verified to run a month without re-sourcing. Niches swing these hard: trades run far hotter than SaaS.

## Where the TAM lives

- Match the source to the niche. LinkedIn-anchored tools cover a fraction of blue-collar, SMB, private, and international markets (HVAC surfaces ~2-4% on Apollo); web-indexed discovery reaches that long tail. Never trust one source to hold a whole market.
- TAM floor: under ~1,000 reachable prospects cold email breaks; pivot the client to ABM or calling.
- For firms under ~10 employees, add public and role emails alongside personal ones.

## Refill

- Decay is physics: reply rates fall across a sequence and re-hitting leads decays them faster. The fix is fresh leads, not copy tweaks; build the refill before you run dry.
- Expansion ladder, chosen by what has already been run: segment harder, then widen a filter or the geo, then mine the client book for profitable sub-verticals, then a new data source, then recycle as a last resort.

## Depth vs breadth

- Thin TAM wins on depth: more enrichment, sharper personalisation, more touches per lead. Wide TAM wins on volume and segmentation, enrichment stays lighter.
- Enrich only fields that change a line. The test for any field is exactly that: does it change a line, or is it vanity data?
