# LinkedIn Outreach Foundations

The craft of writing LinkedIn outbound that books B2B meetings. Tool-agnostic. How this expresses as an Alta campaign lives in `Alta.md`; the deliverable shape lives in `Sequence Interface.md`.

## The channel truth

- **LinkedIn sells on trust, email sells on words.** In the inbox the copy lives or dies alone. On LinkedIn the reader sees the profile, the mutual connections, the background, before a word is read. The message only has to be relevant enough to continue a trust that the profile already started.
- **Targeting and signal do the persuading.** A short, plain message converts when the list is tight and the trigger is real. The same message on a loose list fails. Copy cannot rescue bad targeting; great targeting forgives ordinary copy.
- **You are connection-gated.** Acceptance is the first conversion event. Nothing you write matters until the request is accepted, so acceptance rate is the first number the sequence is built to protect.
- **Volume is small and that is the point.** A profile sends a low daily ceiling of actions. This is a relationship channel run at human scale, not a blast. Win on relevance per touch, not reach.

## The profile is the landing page

You do not build the profile, but you write knowing it is the real first impression. A complete, ICP-aligned sender profile lifts acceptance sharply; a thin one sinks it. Optimize the sender for acceptance, not for how easily it segues into a pitch. When acceptance is low, the profile is usually the cause, not the opener.

## The sequence is an arc

Each touch has one job, in order:

1. **Warm touch** - a profile view and a like on a recent post before the ask. It makes the request land as a person who noticed them, not a bot. Automated where the tool allows.
2. **Connection request** - blank note. A blank request out-accepts a pitched one, and a personalized note is itself a risk-raising signal to the platform. The request is not the place to sell.
3. **Opener** - sent shortly after acceptance. A question, not a pitch. Its only job is to earn a reply.
4. **Follow-ups** - for non-responders. Each delivers value or offers a clean exit. Never a chase.
5. **Breakup** - the last touch. Warm, no pressure, leaves the door open.

## The opener

- **Lead with their context, ask one thing they will answer, and stop.** The reply is the win. The offer waits until a pain is on the table.
- **Phrase it like meeting them at a conference** - a casual fit-check, not a script. "Do you guys handle X yourselves, or is someone dedicated to it?" reads human; a value proposition reads like a pitch.
- **The freshest, most specific signal earns the first line.** A real trigger the reader recognizes is the strongest possible opener. Reference the exact thing - the role they are hiring, the round they raised, the post they wrote - and the message feels one-to-one even when templated.

**Opener archetypes** (proven question shapes; adapt to the offer, keep one idea):
- **The fit-check** - "Do you handle {area} in-house, or does it fall on the team?"
- **The right-person** - "Saw you lead {function} at {company} - are you the one who owns decisions about {category}?"
- **The this-or-that** - "Do you {X}, or do you {Z}?" (forces an easy, revealing answer.)
- **The have-you-heard** - open a curiosity gap tied to their pain, then let them ask.
- **The would-you-consider** - "Would you ever consider {category of solution}?"

**Before and after** (real, corrected):
- *Weak (pitched in touch one, ignored):* "Most leaders assume senior infra has to be a full-time hire, until they see our on-demand model. Happy to share more."
- *Strong (question off the signal):* "Noticed {company} is growing the infra team - curious, is that landing on your engineers, or do you have someone who owns it?"

## The follow-ups

- **Value or exit, never a chase.** A non-responder gets a genuinely useful drop (a relevant example, a short teardown, an observation about their world) or a clean way out. Nothing that reads as needy.
- **Never repeat the ask, never "just checking in."** Banned openings: "following up," "circling back," "just checking in," "did you see my last message." Write like a person, not a sequence.
- **Outcome independence is the tone.** You do not need this one prospect, and the writing shows it. Neediness is what kills the reply on a channel built on status.

## The breakup

Last touch, warm and pressure-free. Acknowledge the silence, leave the door open, wish them well. "Last one from me - if the timing shifts, you know where I am. Either way, rooting for you." A good breakup often reopens a thread a hard ask never would.

## Restraint converts

- Short. One idea per message. If it needs two, it is two messages.
- Casual register. Lowercase energy over corporate polish.
- Enthusiasm dialed to roughly half. A restrained line reads as a peer; an eager one reads as a seller.
- No hard CTA in the sequence. "Would be happy to walk you through it sometime" beats "book a call here."

## Pick a lane

Two modes work; the middle does not.
- **Manual, fully personalized** - a human writing each thread, one to one. Highest quality per touch, lowest volume. The founder-outreach lane.
- **Automated, tight personalized copy** - a fixed sequence or an AI writer, run across a fleet, personalized by signal and merge fields. The scaled lane.
- **The mushy middle kills campaigns** - automated volume with generic copy, or manual effort sprayed thin. Know which lane the campaign is in and write for it. A scaled campaign still earns its relevance from targeting + signal + a curiosity opener, never from generic copy at volume.

## The offer is framed, not invented

The proposition arrives defined. Your job is how it lands: a first line that earns the reply and a soft mention once a pain surfaces, in the reader's terms. Frame the outcome and the contrast ("senior infra on-demand instead of a $250k hire that takes months"), never manufacture claims the offer does not make.

## Benchmarks

Hold the sequence to these; treat them as health, not targets to obsess over:
- Connection acceptance: 20-40% (targeting + profile driven).
- Reply rate off accepted: up to ~35% when dialed.
- Positive-reply rate: ~30% of replies.
- Meeting-book rate: ~35% of positives.
- Rough funnel: 2-5 meetings per 100 connection requests.
- **The one red flag:** any metric near 4% means something is broken - usually targeting or the offer, not the wording. Fix the input, not the sentence.

## The handoff

The sequence owns the thread only until the first reply. The moment a human responds - positive or negative - it is worked, not written, and passes to the inbox manager. Copy's job is to earn the reply; converting it is a different craft.
