## Fast replies (the two-step)

### First Reply — fulfill, then make the call the natural next step
Situation: the owner replied yes to the walkthrough. A teammate had flagged he runs a tool he is happy with (a latent we-are-already-set objection), so the reply fulfills first, then reframes the whole thing onto revenue without ever naming the tool.

> Here you go, John: loom.com/share/91d07d5d2ab54508a2deff9607ba2a3f
>
> Watch it with one number in mind: how many jobs a month you would win if every lead got an instant quote and a follow-up before the other guy called back. For a six-crew LA shop that adds up fast, it is the gap between full trucks and paid days slipping by.
>
> That is what Sam makes real on the call. He ran his own moving company on this and took it from $2M to over $7M doing exactly that. Even if your setup today runs fine, this is about the revenue sitting right next to it.
>
> Next step is getting you with Sam so he maps this onto how your shop actually runs. He's got Monday at 1pm or Tuesday at 2pm PT. Which one works for you?
>
> If neither does, just send me a time that suits and I'll set it up.

Why it wins: fulfills in line one; the reason to call is money he is leaving on the table, tied to his real six-crew LA world; the "even if your setup runs fine" line neutralizes the happy-with-current-tool objection without naming it; the meeting section states the call as the next step, assertive, no hedging, with two times, a real question as the ask, and the own-time invite on its own line. No duration, no calendar link.
(As originally sent, the ask was the bare "Monday 1pm or Tuesday 2pm PT with Sam, which works?" — word-starved and no own-time invite. Corrected above per the First Reply framework.)

### Defer / Not-now — honor it without a push
Situation: a small, medium-fit shop replied check back in a month.

> No problem at all, I will check back in a month.

Why it wins: honors the timing, stays warm, no needy push, no apology for following up. A clean hold that keeps the door open.

## Tasteful follow-ups

### First Follow-up — quiet after a slot offer: assume the meeting, drive it sooner
Situation: a positive owner went quiet after the agent had offered slots, one of which had already passed.

> Hi Stephan,
>
> No reason to sit on this, the sooner Sam walks you through what he changed in his own shop, the sooner it is working for Popeye.
>
> Thursday is no longer open, but Friday 2pm PT just did. Monday 1pm PT works too. Tell me which fits and I will set it up.

Why it wins: assumes the meeting is happening and drives it sooner; quietly drops the dead Thursday and re-anchors two new times with no own-time invite, exactly the First Follow-up slot move; not needy, not apologetic, no exit. (Original said "Want me to lock it?" — lock is banned; corrected.)

### First Follow-up (gateway) — fresh value, then widen the room
Situation: the contact is a gateway (Head of Partnerships), not the owner. Quiet about forty hours after the agent pitched slots that were not real.

> Hey Gus,
>
> Figured the walkthrough gave you the picture, the dispatch board is the part most owners replay.
>
> Where it tends to pay off for a shop your size is upstream of dispatch: every lead off your web quote form, plus the calls and texts, sits in one pipeline and the estimate goes back out in seconds, so none slip while a crew is mid-move. At 50k+ moves that is real money. Cactus runs a 70-93% close rate on qualified jobs that way.
>
> The right room for this is you, whoever runs dispatch, and Sam, so he maps it onto how Mustang actually runs. He is open Friday 12pm or 4pm CT. Which works better?

Why it wins: a fresh value angle the thread had not spent (lead capture, the 70-93% number); real slots; and it widens the room toward the owner and dispatch lead instead of working around the gateway, stated as the right room, not pitched as an idea.

### Next Follow-ups — ghost after a clear yes: do not re-pitch the same ask
Situation: the CEO said yes, we pushed slots twice, then silence. A third identical slot menu would read needy.

> Hey Will,
>
> I know it is busy lately. Quick one while you are mid-season: when a job request comes in off the site today, how fast does a quote usually go back out?
>
> Reason I ask, that gap is where Sam found most of the money when he ran his own shop.

Why it wins: acknowledges he is slammed without apologizing; no third slot menu — the move is a completely relatable question on the exact outcome he replied to, one that, answered, puts us right back in position to offer times. No neediness, no exit.
(As originally sent, this touch offered another slot menu. The Next Follow-ups framework now says: no time slots, get the response. Corrected above.)
