# ICP Definition

A cold-traffic offer is built for a specific person. If the person is fuzzy, the offer is fuzzy. ICP definition is half the work of offer architecture — get it right and the offer almost writes itself; get it wrong and no copy can save it.

A great ICP is *narrow enough that the operator can describe a single specific human* and *broad enough that there are thousands of those humans to sell to.* The skill detects, sharpens, and locks the ICP before the offer is built.

---

## What a complete ICP captures

### 1. Role + decision authority

- **Title / function** — Founder, CEO, Head of Growth, VP Marketing, Director of Sales, Operator.
- **Decision authority** — do they sign the contract, recommend the contract, or only influence it? Cold offers target the *signer*. Recommenders need a different argument.
- **Why this role buys this** — what about their job creates the demand? Different roles have different incentives for the same outcome.

### 2. Company profile

- **Vertical / industry** — B2B SaaS, ecommerce, agency, coaching, real estate. Specific enough that two people in the segment recognize each other.
- **Stage** — pre-revenue / scaling ($0–100K MRR) / scaling ($100K–$1M MRR) / mature ($1M+ MRR). Stage shapes urgency, budget, and willingness to take risk.
- **Size / team** — solo / 2–10 / 10–50 / 50+. Size shapes how the offer gets bought and how DFY scope reads.
- **Geography** — when it matters. Often does not.

### 3. Current state

- **What their day looks like** — concrete activities, tools they already pay for, channels they already run.
- **What they have already tried** — prior agencies, in-house attempts, software they bought. The "graveyard" of past attempts shapes their skepticism.
- **What metrics they already report on** — what they brag about, what they hide. Tells you the dimension of value they care about.

### 4. Pain points (in their words)

- **The daily pain** — the thing that nags them every week.
- **The expensive pain** — the thing costing them real money they can quantify.
- **The status pain** — the thing they are embarrassed about or hiding.
- **The trigger pain** — the thing that, when it spikes, makes them open a search tab.

Pull pain language from real source. Generic pains read as generic offers.

### 5. Desired state

- **What they want their day to look like** — specific, observable.
- **The metric they want to brag about** — the dimension that, if it moved, would change their internal status.
- **What they would do with the time / money / capacity** — the second-order win.

### 6. Awareness level

Per Eugene Schwartz's hierarchy:

- **Unaware** — does not know the problem yet. Demand-generation framing required.
- **Problem-aware** — feels the pain, does not know there is a category of solution.
- **Solution-aware** — knows category solutions exist, has not picked one.
- **Product-aware** — knows specific vendors, comparing.
- **Most-aware** — knows the operator specifically, waiting for the right offer.

ICPs at different awareness levels need different offers. Cold traffic is almost always *problem-aware* or *solution-aware*. Unaware audiences require selling the opportunity itself before the offer.

### 7. Buying triggers

- **External triggers** — events that move them from "later" to "now." New round of funding, hire of a new exec, missed quarter, new compliance deadline, competitor move.
- **Internal triggers** — personal moments. New year. Burnout. Conversation with a peer who's winning.

### 8. Where they live

- **Channels they read** — newsletters, podcasts, LinkedIn voices, slack communities, sub-reddits.
- **Conferences they attend** — physical or virtual.
- **Tools they search** — G2, Capterra, Reddit threads, Twitter.

Useful for outreach later, but also a tell: if the operator can't name three places this person hangs out, the ICP is not specific enough.

## Recognizing the ICP from a client's assets

The skill's unique edge is detecting the right ICP from a client's own data instead of asking the client to define it from scratch. Operators often think their ICP is broader than it is — their best customers cluster more tightly than their stated ICP suggests.

Source signals in priority order:

1. **The client's best existing customers** — names, roles, companies. The pattern in who they have closed before is the strongest ICP signal.
2. **Sales call transcripts** — who books, who closes, who churns. The closed-and-retained group defines the real ICP.
3. **Case studies and testimonials** — who the client points at when proud. These people are the prototype.
4. **Onboarding forms / discovery questions** — the operator's existing intake reveals what they already filter for.
5. **The operator's own content** — who they write *to*, the language they use, the examples they reach for.
6. **Prior outreach** — past campaigns and their reply rates. Who responded matters more than who was targeted.
7. **The operator's stated ICP** — last priority. Often aspirational, not actual.

Cluster the best customers across roles, verticals, stages, situation. The cluster is the real ICP. The cluster's *common pain at the moment of purchase* is the offer's anchor.

### When data is thin

If the client is new, has no customers, or only a handful: pull from concept-proof sources — comparable agencies' case studies, public testimonials in the niche, industry research. Frame the ICP as the prototype the operator wants to close, supported by examples of who similar operators close.

Flag thin data explicitly. ICPs built on aspiration without evidence are weak and produce weak offers.

## Quality test for a finished ICP

A complete ICP passes this test:

- **Specific person test** — the operator can name three real humans in their network who fit the ICP exactly.
- **Volume test** — the operator can estimate there are *at least 1,000* such humans they could reach. Otherwise the ICP is too narrow to support a cold-traffic campaign.
- **Language test** — every pain point uses phrasing the operator has actually heard from this kind of buyer, not invented language.
- **Trigger test** — at least one specific buying trigger is named.
- **Differentiation test** — when the ICP is described to someone in the niche, they instantly know whether they fit. ICPs that produce "maybe me?" reactions are too vague.

If any test fails, sharpen before locking the ICP into the offer.

## Anti-patterns

- **"Anyone who needs more leads."** Not an ICP. A market.
- **Stacking two ICPs into one offer.** "B2B founders *and* marketing directors." Each role needs its own offer. Pick one.
- **Aspirational ICP without evidence.** "I want to sell to F500 CMOs" without a single F500 customer or comparable case study. Flag the aspiration; do not pretend it is reality.
- **Inventing pain language.** Pains pulled from generic templates instead of from the client's actual customer conversations.
- **Job title only.** "CTOs at SaaS companies" without stage, situation, or trigger. Title alone is not an ICP.
