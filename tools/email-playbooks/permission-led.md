# Permission-led

A low-pressure framework that disarms instead of chasing. Give the reader an easy out before you ask for anything, surface the pain with one neutral question, and stay unattached to the outcome the whole way. Nothing to resist, so they answer.

## The play

Lower the stakes, then let them lean in.

- **Permission opener, not a pitch.** Lower the stakes before you raise the ask: "not sure this is even on your radar", "might be off here". The out is the hook.
- **Poke the bear.** One neutral question that makes them reflect on the status quo, never a rhetorical. A small answer is enough to open the thread.
- **Unattached tonality throughout.** Never needy, never a hard ask, never a double-text that chases.
- **The ask comes late.** Only after a signal, offered as a favor, not a close.
- **Follow-ups stay soft.** Each adds a little value or reframes the question, then gives the out again.

## Needs

- A pain nameable in one neutral, non-leading question.
- A single concrete outcome to offer once they lean in.
- Base personalization; a trigger if one is on hand.

## Subject lines

- probably nothing
- quick one {{first_name}}
- off-base?

## Touches

Three touches, each softer than a pitch.

- **Touch 1** - the permission opener and one neutral question. 2 variants.
- **Touch 2** - the question reframed, the out given again. 2 variants.
- **Touch 3** - one light proof and a soft favor-ask. 2 variants.

## Template

**Touch 1**

> Hey {{first_name}},
>
> might be off here, so ignore if so.
>
> {a neutral question about who owns / how they handle {{pain}}}

**Touch 3** - the soft follow-up

> Figured I'd share this either way {{first_name}}.
>
> {one light proof or a reframed question}. Happy to send more if useful, no worries if not.

## Examples

**Permission opener**

> Might be off base here, but curious, when something breaks on the infra side, who actually ends up owning the fix over there?

**Soft follow-up**

> No stress if this isn't a priority.
>
> The reason I asked, most teams your size end up with a developer quietly owning infra until it becomes a problem. Happy to share how a couple of them handed it off, if useful.
