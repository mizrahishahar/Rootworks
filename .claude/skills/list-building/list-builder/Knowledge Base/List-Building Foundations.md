# List-Building Foundations

The doctrine of building cold-email lead lists, pulled from CA coaching calls and coach messages (Matt Lucero, Nick Abraham, Andre Haykal Jr, Stephen Kierans, and others, Apr-Jun 2026).

**Read everything here as a thumb-rule, not a law.** Every number is a directional default and a starting point. List building is contextual: the right answer depends on the client, the offer, the market, and what has already been run against it. There is no fixed recipe. The one line CA repeats: **the list is the lever** — when a campaign is dead, the list is the suspect roughly 80% of the time, not the copy. And you do not get the list right by thinking harder up front; you get it right by sending and reading the data.

---

## 1. The governing mindset

- **Refine the ICP in the inbox, not the spreadsheet.** The first list is a hypothesis. Who replies, who books, who bounces tells you the real ICP. Stephen to Shahar (27 Apr): *"The ICP gets refined through outreach, not before it. You're trying to get certainty before you've sent a single email and that's the bottleneck."* And: *"Stop iterating on the prospect list and send the campaign you built today. That's the only way you get real data."*
- **TAM is directional, not gospel.** Stephen: *"TAM is a directional number not a gospel truth. Every tool spits a different number because they're all estimating... What you're looking for is 'is this big enough to work with' not 'is this exactly right.'"* The reason niches keep looking "too small" is usually filters that are too narrow, not a genuinely tiny market.
- **Count contacts, not companies.** A 1,000-company TAM can be 5,000-10,000 contacts depending on how many people you target per company. That alone can flip a "too small" niche into a viable one.
- **Define the ICP just tight enough that one specific copy line is true for everyone on the list.** That is the real test of "narrow enough," not a TAM threshold.

## 2. Sizing a list and a test

All figures are CA's live defaults — directional starting points, not targets to hit exactly.

**Test and segment sizing**
- **~1,500-2,500 leads per campaign test** is the commonly quoted sweet spot. Fewer and the data lies ("small batches lie" — Andre); more and you have over-committed before knowing the angle works.
- **~1,000-2,000 leads per native segment** when you segment, each one native to that segment.
- **~5,000+ total touches** before judging "is this working" (e.g. 1,500 leads × a 3-step sequence).
- **2,000-20,000 leads per campaign** is the standard cohort range Matt quotes; most campaigns sit in that band. Below a couple thousand is painful to spin up; much larger should be chunked.
- **2-4 concurrent campaigns per client** is typical — segmented cohorts run in parallel, hypothesis-driven, not one mega-list.

**Send volume before you read anything**
- **5,000-10,000 sends before you judge a campaign at all.** Below that is noise. Matt: forget leads, watch the sent number, *at least 5,000 before I make any decisions.*
- **~1,000 emails/day for ~7 days** is the minimum viable validation run for a trial. Nick: you can validate an offer in 7 days at 1,000/day; no interested lead in that window means kill or pivot.
- Daily sending is gated by inbox count, not list size. The list just has to keep up. Rough inbox math: ~1 mailbox per domain sustainably; a Google inbox sends ~15-20/day, so ~290 inboxes ≈ ~5,000/day ≈ ~90,000/month.

**Back-of-envelope: how big a list to hit N calls**
Using the directional baselines (~1 meeting per 5,000 sends; ~1 positive per 750; ~20% of positives convert to a booked call):
- ~5,000 sends ≈ ~1 meeting (sometimes 2-4 per 1,000 in stronger niches — it swings hard by market).
- To sustain a month of experiments without constantly re-sourcing, a cohort of **~10,000-15,000 verified leads** is a sane starting point, recycled rather than replaced (see §6).

## 3. Reading list health (diagnostics)

Directional baselines for whether a list is the problem. Niche-dependent — trades and blue-collar often run far hotter than B2B SaaS; treat these as the "B2B-normal" band, not universal lines.

| Signal | Rough baseline | What it suggests |
|---|---|---|
| Bounce rate | under ~3% | above it, suspect list quality (or infra), not copy |
| Reply rate (any) | ~1% | directionally viable |
| Positive replies | ~1 per 750 sent | below it after 5k sends is concerning |
| Meetings | ~1 per 5,000 sent | the golden ratio to back-calc list size |
| Confidence | 10-15 positives | before you trust an angle |

**Triage — list vs copy vs infra (Matt, 26 May):** *"Diagnosing low metrics: lead list is the culprit 80% of the time. Low reply rate = infra or lead list. Low positive reply rate = lead list or copy. Default to questioning the list first."* List is the most-slapped-on fix because it is most often the real one.

**Catch-all rate is niche-relative.** A high catch-all share (e.g. ~30%) on an SMB-hosting B2B list is normal — the "hosting tax," not a quality failure. Different niches have different catch-all baselines; read it against the niche, not an absolute.

## 4. Segmentation strategy

CA's current stance: **segmentation now beats AI personalization.** Slice the list into native segments and write copy that is true for that whole segment; that out-pulls spray-and-pray AI personalization on a broad list. The point of a segment is that one specific line is honest for everyone in it: *"I noticed you're an IT firm, we help IT firms get this result in this timeframe with this mechanism."* Specificity is the weapon; the segment is what makes specificity honest.

Levers (pick by context, not by formula):
- **One layer deeper** — niche by vertical, location, or job title tied directly to the value prop. *"I saw you're a director of marketing, are you hitting your [number]?"* beats generic.
- **Persona vs industry depends on company size (Nick).** Persona-level call-outs tend to land only at larger companies (~200+ employees). Below that, industry-subcategory call-outs (FinTech, healthcare-tech, etc.) tend to do better than persona ones. Thumb-rule, not a wall.
- **Tool / integration affinity** — target users of a specific stack (HubSpot, Salesforce, a vertical tool) and lead with integration messaging. Maps to Discolike's tech_stack filter.
- **Offer-viability sub-type** — the surface niche is not the segment. "Recruiters" splits into SDR-staffing vs dev-staffing vs corporate, each with wildly different cold-email viability (Nick). Segment by what actually makes the offer land.
- **Real social proof needs real segments** — to say "here's the result we got for another supplement brand," you must build a supplement-brand list and campaign. AI personalization cannot fake segment-specific proof.
- **Hypothesis-driven cohorts** — pick 2-4 segments you believe will perform, run them in parallel, let results decide. Not random, not exhaustive.
- **Mine the client's own book** — a client's existing customer/CRM list, clustered, reveals their most profitable sub-verticals (often ones they cannot name themselves). Each becomes a new, tightly-defined ICP to expand into. (Mechanics: [[DiscoLike]].)
- **Use the subcategory variable, not the generic one** (Nick Abraham, backed by 15M+ emails) — the highest-leverage relevance is usually naming the prospect's *specific* subcategory rather than the umbrella category: a "{{plumbing / roofing / HVAC}} contractor," not just "a contractor." For the list builder this is a sourcing instruction more than a copy one — capture the subcategory as a field on the list (via discovery filters or DiscoGen research), one level below the obvious category, so the opening line can be specifically true. Cheap to add, compounds in performance.

This ties to Matt's three levers that make campaigns convert: Blue-Ocean offer, Very-Specific targeting/copy, No-Brainer CTA. Segmentation is how you buy the "very specific" lever. Two of three crushes; one is okay; zero is terrible.

## 5. Where the TAM actually lives (coverage)

The most under-appreciated list-building skill: knowing which database truly holds a niche. **LinkedIn-anchored tools (Apollo, ZoomInfo, AI Arc, and the LinkedIn-scrape contact databases) only cover a fraction of most markets** and collapse on blue-collar, SMB, private, and international companies, because those firms have thin or no LinkedIn presence.

Real coverage gaps the coaches cite:
- HVAC: ~100k companies exist, only ~2-4k surface on Apollo (2-4%).
- Political organizations: ~10k exist, Apollo returned ~800 (8%).
- Movers, roofers, trades: very thin on LinkedIn-based contact data.

**Web-indexed discovery reaches the long tail.** Discolike indexes companies from SSL certificates — essentially every business with a secure website, ~70M domains, claimed ~3× LinkedIn coverage. This is exactly where it earns its keep: the niches LinkedIn tools go blind on. (Matt: *"Disco somehow found a way to index literally every single company on the internet that has an SSL."*)

Judgment that follows:
- **Never trust one source to hold a whole market.** Match the source to the niche: web-indexed discovery for blue-collar/SMB/long-tail; LinkedIn-based contact data where the buyers are genuinely on LinkedIn; niche directories for odd verticals (ask an LLM "what databases list X").
- **TAM floor.** Under ~1,000 reachable prospects, the cold-email model generally breaks — pivot the client to ABM or cold calling. (Matt's industrial-equipment-leasing example: ~800 prospects = not list-able at volume.)
- **Geo expansion is a TAM lever.** Less-saturated markets (LATAM, Nordics) often reply more; adjust the company-size band per region's buying power (e.g. 10+ employees in the US, 20-30+ in LATAM) so you are not hitting firms that cannot pay.
- **Public vs personal email path.** For local companies and firms under ~10 employees, default to add public/role emails (info@, sales@, an owner-name pattern) in addition to personal ones.

**Lookalikes versus structured filters: when each.** Seed-domain lookalikes (Discolike ranks its whole index by similarity to your seeds) are a similarity bet — only as strong as the representativeness of the seeds and the coherence of the vertical. Lean on lookalikes when you have genuinely representative exemplars (the client's won deals, textbook-fit customers), when the vertical is too fuzzy to pin with structured filters, or when expanding from a campaign that already converts ("more like these"). Lean on structured filters instead (industry, size, geo, tech) when the niche is cleanly definable, when your seeds are not representative (a few off-ICP seeds bias the entire ranked pull toward the wrong cluster), or when the market is so saturated or fragmented that "similar" stops discriminating (Matt's CPA example — thousands of near-identical firms, where competitor-lookalike finds everyone and so finds no one). Always replace Discolike's auto-suggested seeds with your own when you have better ones.

## 6. Iteration, refill, and the expansion ladder

- **Decay is physics, not failure.** Reply rates fall naturally across a sequence (email 1 > 2 > 3), and re-hitting the same leads decays them faster. Matt: *"The fix isn't tactics, it's new leads."* Iteration means a fresh supply pipeline, not endless copy tweaks.
- **Refresh triggers:** either current campaigns degrade in performance, or they run out of leads. Expect to launch new campaigns a couple of times a month per active client.
- **Build the refill before you run dry.** The model only works if you can keep sourcing. Plan the next pull while the current list still sends.
- **The expansion ladder** — when a TAM tires, choose the next lever by what has already been run against this offer:
  1. **Segment harder** — carve a fresh native segment out of the same market.
  2. **Widen** — loosen a filter, add adjacent industry groups, extend the geo or size band.
  3. **Mine the client's book** — cluster their customers to surface untapped profitable sub-verticals.
  4. **New data source** — move to a different database where more of the TAM lives.
  5. **Recycle / re-touch (last resort)** — only when fresh supply is exhausted, lean on frequency (recycling the same list across more touches over 2-3 month gaps). Fresh contacts beat re-touching old ones.
- **Fit-check before scaling.** Sample a small pull and read the keep-rate before committing to a large one; a high keep-rate confirms the query definition is good.
- **Database exhaustion is real.** If the contact-generation waterfall returns nothing for a set of companies, those firms are genuinely below scraping visibility — accept the gap or go manual (LinkedIn-by-hand). Do not keep re-running the same pull hoping for a different result.

**The email-to-lead ratio: which list move to make** (Nick Abraham). One metric frames the decision — the volume a campaign needs against the leads its TAM can reach (how hard each lead is being worked). Benchmark a campaign against the agency norm, read it together with the client's nature and ICP breadth (one vertical or many), then take one of three paths:
1. **At or above average** — the offer works. Top off with net-new leads using the same targeting plus lookalikes, enriched and validated before they hit the campaign. *List move: keep feeding.*
2. **Underperforming by ~40%+ and you can move verticals** — do not add leads to a broken campaign; it just burns contacts. Take the same offer into a new, more relevant vertical and launch fresh. If the audience still does not respond after enough volume, it is a fit problem, not a copy problem. *List move: a fresh list in a new vertical.*
3. **Underperforming, locked into one vertical, offer will not change** — no list can save it; you are down to copy and angle iteration, the lowest-probability path, and you tell the client that directly. *List move: none — name it.*

This is the frame for deciding not just how to build a list, but whether building one is even the right move.

## 7. Client list-ability (qualification)

Before building, judge whether a buildable, worthwhile list even exists for this client. Matt qualifies on three things — TAM, ACV, and company size:
- **TAM** big enough to send high volume and to expand into over time.
- **ACV** high enough that the math works — ideally selling something worth >$20k. High ACV forgives low meeting counts (a client who paid ~$60k for <10 meetings stayed because one deal was worth ~$500k-$1M). Low ACV punishes you even when you book plenty.
- **Company size** — roughly >10 employees as a floor; the sweet spot is ~$5-100M revenue: small enough to move fast, big enough that your fee is insignificant, with a TAM large enough to expand.

Two more judgment calls:
- **Start-the-fire vs pour-gas.** A client with a working motion (existing meetings, proven angles) is "pour gas" — easy to add to. A client with zero traction and no product-market fit is "start the fire" — far harder; frame them as a market-testing engagement, not guaranteed lead-gen, and set expectations explicitly.
- **Your standards are not the client's standards.** A "mediocre" result can delight a high-ACV client; a "great" result can leave a tiny client neurotic. Pick clients accordingly; list quality cannot fix a mismatch in expectations.

## 8. Depth versus breadth — what to enrich

A list wins on volume or on depth, rarely both, and the reachable TAM decides which. This is a core list-builder responsibility: not just who is on the list, but what information each row carries.

- **Thin TAM → win on depth.** When the reachable universe is small (low email-to-lead headroom), you cannot spray. The maneuver room moves into the fields: more enrichment, sharper per-lead personalization, more touches per lead. Get the absolute most relevance the available data and budget allow.
- **Wide TAM → win on volume and segmentation.** When the universe is large, the native segment line already carries the relevance; enrichment stays lighter, and you lean on reach and recycling.
- **What to enrich is judgment per list, never a fixed schema.** Beyond name, title, and company, ask one question: which one or two researched fields would make the opening line specifically true for *this* list? The subcategory variable (see §4) is usually the first and cheapest. Others worth their cost: a tool or vendor they use, a recent growth or hiring signal, a specific service line, a location hook. The test is always the same — does this field change a line, or is it vanity data? Collect only fields that change a line.
- **Read the maneuver room.** The craft of enrichment is reading how much relevance the reachable data and the client's budget actually allow, and extracting the best list that supports, not the ideal list the data cannot.

*Remember: thumb-rules, not laws. Read every number above against the actual client, offer, market, and history in front of you. The judgment is the job.*
