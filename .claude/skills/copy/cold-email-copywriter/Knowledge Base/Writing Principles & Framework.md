# Writing Principles & Framework

This file is the whole craft: how to read a situation, the foundations that always hold, and the five-part framework with the tools and dials you choose from. Read all of it before you write, every time. Nothing here is strict. It is a kit, not a recipe. Your job is to read the specific situation and pick the tools that fit it, then iterate.

---

# ★ READ THE SITUATION FIRST

The same offer wants different copy in a fresh niche than in a hammered one, on a first campaign than on a third, on a cold list than on a recycle. You cannot write the best copy without knowing the situation. So before a single line, establish it.

**Know the campaign context** (pull it from the brief; if it is missing, ask):
- **Stage** - is this the first campaign for this segment, or a 2nd / 3rd? A later campaign must not repeat angles that already ran.
- **Type** - a D100 1:1 deliverable (hyper-personalized to one prospect) or a segment campaign (one line true for the whole list)? They are written differently.
- **Recycle** - are we sending again to a list already contacted? If so, a new angle is mandatory. Never the prior script.
- **Past results** - if this segment or offer has run before, what the data said: what pulled replies, what died. Build on it, do not repeat what failed.

**Read the market** (this is what sets the dials below):
- **Awareness** - do they already know this solution category exists? If not, reach for the unaware-market opener and a status-quo pitch.
- **Saturation** - how hammered is this inbox with the same pitch? The more saturated, the more you need a mechanism, a sharper angle, or a harder-to-ignore freebie.
- **Niche sophistication** - a sophisticated B2B buyer wants numbers and proof; a local trade wants a plain offer and no overclaiming.
- **Company size** - persona call-outs land at ~200+ employees; below that, name the company and what they do.
- **Proof on hand** - marquee names, a matched case study, or nothing (then risk reversal carries the proof slot).
- **List tightness and language** - how segmented and personalizable the list is; for local non-English markets, native-language copy can pull several times the replies.

**The dials you set from this read** (each detailed in its part below):

| Dial | Default | Main exception |
|---|---|---|
| Quantify | Number + guarantee, unmissable | Low-sophistication trades: strip it |
| Subject personalization | On (first name, company) | Low-reply verticals (e-comm): drop {{company}} |
| CTA friction | Soft ask | Seed-the-deliverable / harder ask when intent or saturation is high |
| Mechanism | Omit, lead with the outcome | Add when the outcome reads skeptical or the market is oversaturated |
| Proof type | Matched case study | Marquee names in low-trust niches; competitor-proof where competitors are nameable |
| Personalization depth | Segmentation | Native-language for local markets; persona only at 200+ |

We always reach for numbers and guarantees unless the situation is one of the exceptions.

## THE WINNING FORMULA

The distinction between copy that gets 93 positive replies and copy that gets 5:

**Winning (93 replies):**
> "We can create branded apparel for {{company_name}} - from top brands like Peter Millar, Patagonia, and Travis Mathew.
>
> You'll reduce your apparel costs by up to 20% - in fact, we guarantee you'll reduce your unit product costs or else you don't have to pay us.
>
> Mind if I share a video going over our apparel catalog?"

**Losing (5 replies):**
> "Curious, are you noticing the rising costs for {{company_name}}'s branded merchandise?
>
> We can help you save 25 percent on your merch costs. You'll pay a flat monthly fee, get complete cost transparency, and only 10% markup on all products.
>
> Recently helped a 1bn dollar company cut their premium apparel costs by over 20%. Worth a quick chat?"

The winner is shorter, pitches directly, names real brands the prospect cares about, carries a guarantee in plain words, and offers a value CTA. The loser is longer, opens with a vague question, uses generic language, and asks for time while giving nothing. When in doubt, ask: would the winner do this?

## THE FOUNDATIONS

These ten hold in every situation. The dials and tools flex; these do not.

### Principle 1: Short wins
Cold emails are read in under 5 seconds. Aim under ~50 words for the body; many winners land near 35. Treat ~70 as a hard ceiling. If you can cut a word without losing meaning, cut it.

### Principle 2: Speak to the prospect, never about the category
Write to one specific person. "We specialize in bookkeeping for ecommerce brands" becomes "We can take your entire bookkeeping off your plate". The test: if the line could sit on your homepage, it is wrong. Naming a tight segment is fine when it is specific; generic homepage copy is not.

### Principle 3: Specific language, and one predefined outcome
Name brands, technical terms, certifications, product types. "We sell premium scrubs" becomes "We supply Figs Body Sets to hospitals at 30% below retail". And pitch one predefined, specific outcome, never open-ended capability: "I can do AI for you" or "I can do anything" is too broad and dies because the reader cannot picture it. Productize one concrete result they can see.

### Principle 4: No vague benefit padding
If the benefit is implied by the offer, do not spell it out. "...so you always know your real margins" - cut it. "P&Ls, tax filings, and inventory accounting" is enough.

### Principle 5: Value-based, personal CTAs
The CTA sells the reply. Make it feel already done: "I built you a sample landing page to get a feel for our work" beats "Want to see a sample?". The pattern: "I {{verb}} {{specific thing}} for {{company_name}}". Lead-magnet CTAs are one clean line: what it is, who it is for, the result it delivers.

### Principle 6: Write like a real person, sent from a phone
One person to another, typed from a phone. Not marketing copy. "Looking to optimize your inbound lead generation funnel?" becomes "Are you getting inbound calls from your LinkedIn?".
- No links, no images, no open tracking. They read as a pitch and hurt deliverability.
- No bold, and no em dashes. Both read as marketing or AI-generated. Plain sentences.
- Avoid spam-trigger words and stacked symbols: multiple dollar signs, ALL CAPS, "millionaire", "act now". When unsure, run the copy through a spam-word checker.

The test: would you say it out loud at a coffee shop? If no, rewrite.

### Principle 7: Variants are genuinely different, not reworded
Each variant tests a real angle: a different subject family, opener, body structure, proof, or CTA. Not everything must differ in every variant; use judgment. Four variants that say the same thing four ways are template syndrome. Repeat your strongest selling point across variants, but say it a different way each time.

### Principle 8: Openers are specific, not vague
Specific "how I found you" openers work; vague ones fail. "Came across {{company}} while looking for google ads solutions - really impressed by your case studies" works; "I came across your profile and wanted to reach out" fails. Avoid entirely: "Most {{industry}} struggle with...", "Are you tired of...", "Imagine if...", "I hope this email finds you well...".

### Principle 9: Never invent case studies, numbers, or clients
Never fabricate a client name, statistic, dollar amount, case study, or percentage. If the brief provides proof, use it. If not, risk reversal carries the proof slot. People's jobs depend on accuracy.

### Principle 10: Personalization placeholders
When personalization depends on per-prospect research, use descriptive placeholders so the operator knows where to plug in: `{{product_types}}`, `{{specific_product}}`, `{{recent_initiative}}`.

# ◆ THE FRAMEWORK: THE FIVE PARTS

> This is the spine of every cold email you write. The foundations are the craft; these five parts are the structure that craft fills. Master the five parts, their tools, and their dials, and you can write any sequence for any situation.

Every cold email is five parts: **subject, opener, body, CTA, and the follow-ups after it.** The parts are fixed; what goes in each is a fresh choice for the situation. It is a menu, not a template: two sequences for two situations should look meaningfully different because the choices are different.

### Subject line
One job: earn the click. Not where you sell. Look natural, do not trip spam filters, do not overthink it. Two families, mixed across a first email's variants so the data tells you which the segment responds to:
- **Natural / internal** (lowest friction; default for cold or senior): `Quick question {{first_name}}` / `Thoughts, {{first_name}}?` / `Question about {{company}}` / `{{company}} <> [you] - intro`
- **Outcome / benefit** (number or guarantee up front; use when the offer is strong): `More sales calls for {{company}}` / `20% more conversions, guaranteed` / `55k in sales or you don't pay`

Keep it short; live personalization helps. No stacked symbols, no all-caps, no hype. Follow-ups thread under the first email with no new subject.

**Dial - personalization:** personalize by default. Exception: in low-reply verticals like e-commerce, drop the {{company}} variable, it can hurt more than help. Test it.

### Opener
The first line, shown next to the subject. Break the ice, ease toward the pitch, keep it about them. **Ask, do not lecture** - a question pulls; a statement that tries to educate a stranger pushes them away (you are a stranger in their inbox, not a YouTube video they chose to watch). Keep every opener **plain, not cleverly worded** - the plain observation beats the clever noun phrase ("your wedge is branding for FinTech" reads worse than just naming it).

Framing patterns:
- **Customer-disguise** (local / consumer): "Was searching for a trusted {{niche}} in {{city}} and came across {{company}}..."
- **Referral / raving** (ecommerce / consumer): "A friend was raving about {{company}}'s {{product}}, had to check it out myself..."
- **Found-while-searching + proof**: "Came across {{company}} while looking for {{category}} - really impressed by your case studies."
- **Role call-out** (~200+ employees): "Noticed you're a key player at {{company}} - I imagine you're after {{outcome}}."
- **Founder-assumption**: "As the founder of a {{niche}} company, I bet you're always looking to {{outcome}}."

Personalized openers - tools you reach for when the data hands you the hook:
- **Tech-stack**: "Saw you're running {{Salesforce}} and {{Asana}} - would you be open to..."
- **New hire**: "How's {{name}} settling into the {{role}} seat? Saw you just brought them on." A pattern-interrupt that earns "this is clever" replies.
- **Employee departure**: "Saw {{name}} left {{company}} in {{month}} - are you getting any feedback from ex-employees?"
- **Growth signal**: "Saw {{company}} is hiring a lot internationally..." Lead with the firmographic signal.
- **Proximity** (local / field service): "We'll be down the street on another {{job}} this week, happy to have the team stop by."
- **Unaware-market status-quo** (high-leverage when you have the info): name their current painful status quo as a question, then introduce the alternative. "Are you still paying a scribe to sit in on multilingual patient visits?" Use this when the prospect does not yet know the solution category exists.

### Body
Sells the reply, not the whole service. Short, specific, to them. Use "you" far more than "we" or "I" (count them, aim "you" at least 5x over we/I): they are the hero with the gap, you are the guide who closes it. It carries: the gap made concrete (the number), a mechanism only if needed, and proof or risk reversal.

**Pitch one specific, predefined outcome** (Principle 3). The reader has to be able to picture it.

**Split when it runs long.** Keep the body to short lines. If it carries more than one thought, break it into two lines, one thought each, never a wall of text. A two-line body reads in a glance; a paragraph gets skipped.

Structure menu (prospect-facing voice):
- **Direct**: "We can get you {{quantified outcome}} - without {{their objection}}."
- **Case-study-led**: "We can {{outcome}} for you, just like we did for {{named client}}."
- **System-named**: "We can bring you {{number}} {{result}} through our {{named}} system."
- **Question-led**: "What if {{desired outcome}} every month? We {{mechanism}} without you {{pain}}."
- **Responsibility-split (DFY)**: "We script it, edit it, and post it - you just film." Spells out what you do vs what they do; reads as effortless for them.
- **Ego / status**: "We'll make you the go-to {{authority}} in your space - {{number}} {{result}}." Frames the outcome around their status.
- **Seasonal hook**: "With the holidays coming up, want a free {{mockup}} for your team?" Ties the pitch to a moment.

**Dial - quantify:** Default, surface the number and the guarantee and make them unmissable. Exception, low-sophistication trades (plumbers, pallet companies, local services): strip it, no number, timeframe, or guarantee needed; the niche plus low competition is the differentiator. Name the trade plainly: "We're a pallet company - want a quote?" Tell: a fragmented local market with no nameable competitors, where the buyer is not comparing claims.

**Dial - mechanism:** Default, lead with the outcome, not the how (you are selling the reply, not explaining the service). Add a mechanism only when the outcome reads too-good or skeptical, or the market is oversaturated with the same claim, and only if the mechanism is interesting or can be framed as interesting. "I can refresh your YouTube thumbnails to lift your traffic" earns the reply where "I can add 50k subscribers" gets ignored. Never a vague or AI-slop mechanism ("our 1-2-3 system").

**Dial - proof type:** Marquee-name testimonials lead in low-trust niches (coaching, executive, advisory) - name the big clients in the email. Competitor-proof ("here's what we did for {{brand}} - saw you're a competitor") works where competitors are nameable; both die in fragmented markets where the prospect recognizes no names. Never name the prospect's direct competitor as your client, it backfires. Personal credibility is also proof: "I was the lead AE for {{org}} - bringing that experience to you."

Rules:
- **The freebie must pass "why would I not say yes to this?"** A tangible, specific freebie works; generic audits and "satisfaction guarantee" are dead on arrival.
- **No bullet lists in service copy.** Bullets are fine only when selling a physical product. For a service, write plain conversational lines.
- **Do not hardcode a segment word** ("SaaS", "roofers") if the list bleeds past the ICP, name the pain, not the label.
- If a real case study exists, name it. If not, risk reversal carries the proof slot ("on a pay-per-call basis", "or you don't pay").

### CTA
Sells the reply, points to a real asset, feels already done. Map the assets you actually have first, then spread them across variants. A CTA is a tool, pick the one that fits; none is the permanent default.

Menu:
- Soft asks: "Mind if I share more info?" / "Would that be something {{company}} can benefit from?" / "Would you be open for a quick chat?" / "Thoughts on this approach for {{company}}?"
- "Can I share a quick case study on how we helped {{similar company}}?"
- **Seed-the-deliverable**: "Can I show you 5 {{influencers / leads / ideas}} I already pulled for {{company}}?" Handing over a piece of the real work, almost impossible to say no to.
- **Risk-reversal CTA**: "We can do this on a full tech fee plus cost-per-lead - so there's no risk to you."
- **Video / Loom**: one option, not the default. It carries more friction and often goes unwatched. Reach for it when you have nothing more tangible, not as the standard ask.

**Dial - friction:** soft asks book more replies at lower intent; seed-the-deliverable and harder asks book fewer at higher intent. Choose by saturation and how much proof you carry.

Rules:
- Never a calendar link; never a specific-time ask on the first email unless the brief says the CTA is a call.
- **Never promise a CTA you cannot fulfill.** "Can I send you the list?" with no list to send burns the reply.

### Follow-ups
The sequencer writes the follow-up emails (responses to replies belong to the inbox manager, not here). Persistent, never repeating, never re-introducing (no "following up", "circling back", "touching base"). Three moves:
- **Rephrase** - the same pitch a different way; maybe the first framing did not land.
- **Email-tree** - "Who at {{company}} is the best person to discuss {{outcome}}?" Restates what you do while routing you to the decision-maker.
- **Value-add** - give something they did not get the first time: a bigger case study, a stronger number, a named asset.

---

## BUILDING VARIANTS

The first email carries the most variants because that is where you test angles. Each variant is a genuinely different angle (Principle 7). Two ways to build the spread:
- **Vary the angle** - a different subject family, opener tool, body structure, proof, or CTA per variant.
- **Length ladder** (one option) - start with one very short variant, then make each next variant slightly longer, each adding one thing (one adds the mechanism, one adds a second proof point), while keeping one element constant across all (often the guarantee). It tests how much copy this segment actually needs.

## SELF-CHECK BEFORE OUTPUTTING

Run the sequence against these. Any "no" gets rewritten.

1. Did I read the situation first (stage, recycle, awareness, saturation, niche, size, proof) and set the dials from it?
2. Is each body short (aim under ~50 words, never padded) and split into short lines when it runs long?
3. Am I speaking to "you" with a specific, predefined outcome, not a generic category claim?
4. Is there specific industry language: brands, technical terms, real product types?
5. Did the subject lines look natural, mix families across the first email's variants, and respect the personalization dial?
6. Did each opener carry a specific, plain (not clever), believable hook, with no banned patterns?
7. Is each CTA value-based or a soft ask, personal, matched to a real asset I can fulfill, with no calendar link, and is the friction level right for the situation?
8. Are the first email's variants genuinely different angles, not one email reworded?
9. Did follow-ups avoid "following up" and never re-introduce?
10. Did I set the quantify dial right (number + guarantee, or stripped for a trade) and the proof dial right for the niche's trust level?
11. Did I only use case studies the brief explicitly gave me?
12. Does it sound like a real person talking, with no bullet lists in service copy, no links, and no bold or em dashes?
