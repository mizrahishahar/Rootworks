# Ty Frankel Campaign Method

*For Katie: this is the philosophy of a proven LinkedIn client-acquisition operator. Use it to shape how a message and a sequence should feel. It was built for manual one-to-one outreach, so the daily-ritual parts describe how a human runs it; what you apply is the craft, the way each message is written and sequenced. Do not invent Ty's numbers or claims beyond what is written here.*

## The shape of the funnel

Connection requests are the engine. The connection request can be automated; the messaging never should feel automated. Aim for a high acceptance rate by looking like someone worth connecting with, then treat each accept as a real conversation, not a slot in a sequence. A booked call comes from a handful of genuine conversations, not from blasting.

## Warm before the message

Before the first message, act like a person who noticed them: view the profile, like a post or two, leave one real comment. The warm touches are what make the first message read as human rather than as automation.

## The conversation, not the pitch

- **Short, multi-thread messages, one idea each** - the way a person actually texts, not one dense block.
- **Casual register.** Restrained, not eager. A compliment lands at about half-volume ("solid case study, 3.2x is strong for that space"), never "I absolutely love this."
- **Mirror, resonate, reflect, then ask** - respond to what they actually said before moving forward. Never a robotic question-answer-question rhythm.
- **Drop authority-building observations, not pitches** - a real insight about their world or a piece of proof, offered, not sold.
- **Pry the pain gently.** The pain usually shows up small; a good question opens it wider. You are finding whether there is a real fit, not forcing one.

## The "what do you mean?" move

The cheat code: name a specific idea tied to their pain in a way that makes them ask what you mean. They ask, you explain, you tie it to a common problem, you offer proof, and only then a soft mention of a conversation. The pitch language stays non-needy: "would be happy to run you through it sometime," never "let me know if you want to book a call."

## Assets as a gesture

A useful asset (a short case study, a doc, a quick teardown) works as a friendly-helper signal, mostly the gesture of giving, dropped mid-conversation or in a follow-up. It is help, not a lead magnet dressed as help.

## Follow-ups

For non-responders, deliver value or foreshadow something genuinely relevant. Never "just checking in." The whole thing runs on outcome independence: you do not need this one person, and that is exactly what keeps every message non-needy.

## How Katie applies this

The transferable craft, campaign by campaign: open with curiosity or a fit-check question off the signal, keep messages short and one-idea, stay restrained and non-needy, prefer an observation or a question over a pitch, let a soft mention of a conversation come only after a pain surfaces, and make every follow-up give something. The manual daily volume and hand-commenting are a founder's way of running it; the writing craft above is what a campaign should embody.
