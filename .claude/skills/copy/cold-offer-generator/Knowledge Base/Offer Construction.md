# Offer Construction

The mechanics of building a cold-traffic offer. An offer is the proposition expressed in one sentence — the single sentence that determines whether a stranger gives the operator their attention.

---

## Offer vs Service vs Value Proposition

Three distinct things. Not interchangeable.

- **Service** = what the operator does. "Video editing." "Cold email." "Done-for-you ads."
- **Value Proposition** = what the product delivers + the result. Internal language. *"We run outbound so you get more booked calls."*
- **Cold Traffic Offer** = the value proposition reformulated through the Fazio pillars so a cold stranger has a reason to respond. *"We book 8–12 qualified calls per month for B2B SaaS founders without them touching it — or we refund every cent."*

The offer is built on top of the value prop. They are distinct. Cold traffic never buys services. It buys offers.

---

## Why people buy

There are only three categories of buying motivation: **health, love, wealth.** In B2B, every buyer is in the wealth category. The only reason a B2B client buys is the belief that the service will make them richer.

Every component of a working offer serves that single belief.

> Promise a specific result, then make the prospect confident that result is actually achievable.

---

## The three pillars of the offer

The logical sequence prospects ask in their head, in order:

1. **Claim** — what result will I get?
2. **Guarantee** — what happens if it does not work?
3. **Evidence** — who else has this worked for?

Rules:
- If there is no evidence, both claim and guarantee are required.
- If evidence is overwhelming, the guarantee can be implicit.
- A claim with neither evidence nor guarantee is just noise.

This is the *prospect's* test for an offer. It overlaps with — but is distinct from — the Fazio Three Pillars, which are the *operator's* test for cold-traffic viability.

---

## The Claim: quantifiable end results

Claims come in dimensions. For any service, ask why the client would buy it. Each reason is a dimension. Each dimension can be quantified.

Example — a content agency could promise:
- Views
- Followers
- Leads
- Sales calls booked
- Clients signed
- Sales and revenue

The further right on the dimension chain, the closer to money. The closer to money, the more attractive the claim. *"10 new clients guaranteed"* beats *"10,000 new followers guaranteed."*

The number must sit in "paying attention distance" of the buyer. A claim that is too small gets ignored by people with budget. Pulling punches on the claim — quoting safe, small numbers because the operator is afraid they cannot deliver — is the most common reason offers fail. Filter unqualified buyers on the call, not on the headline.

### What is NOT a quantifiable result

- Pipeline
- Opportunities
- "Leads" without a number
- "Better X." "More Y." "Improved Z." "Faster growth."

These are placeholders, not claims.

## The Guarantee

Cold traffic conversion rates are inversely proportional to perceived risk. Guarantees collapse risk. The right guarantee multiplies booked calls 2–4×.

### Types (least risky for buyer → most)

| Type | Description | Buyer risk |
|---|---|---|
| Performance basis | Buyer pays only as results occur | Zero |
| Money-back | Buyer is refunded if results do not occur | Small (operator must honor) |
| Implicit | Overwhelming evidence carries the promise | Higher; only viable with major authority |

### Phrasing for cold traffic

Headlines carry the marketable shape, not the legal terms. Front-end copy is the simplest, most attractive truthful version. Stipulations, refund tiers, and definitions live in the contract.

- **Bad:** *"Performance-based lead gen where we book sales calls and refund the calls we cannot book if you maintain a 15% close rate and provide call recordings."*
- **Good:** *"Performance-based lead gen — you only pay for qualified sales calls that show up."*

### Stipulations exist to protect the operator

Stipulations are conditions the buyer must meet to qualify for the guarantee.

- **State stipulations** — the buyer must already have X. ("Must currently send a minimum of 5,000 emails/month.")
- **Action stipulations** — the buyer must do X. ("Must show up to all weekly check-in calls.")

Stipulations belong in the contract, not the headline. The headline carries the most attractive truthful claim. Stipulations protect against refund abuse without weakening the offer.

### "What if I cannot deliver to everyone?"

The guarantee is for qualified buyers only. Unqualified buyers do not get sold the same offer on the call. The headline must be strong enough to attract the qualified buyer in the first place — without that, the operator never gets the chance to qualify anyone.

## Transfer of logistical intensity

Clients pay for the transfer of work from their plate to the operator's. The more logistical intensity captured, the more justifiable the price.

Two practical implications for the offer:

1. **List every deliverable.** Naming twenty steps justifies $10,000. Naming three steps justifies $1,000. An offer that under-describes deliverables underprices itself in the prospect's head.
2. **Going deep beats stacking.** Adding many shallow services ("email *and* ads *and* SEO *and* SMM") makes the offer look like a shopping cart. Going one layer deeper on a single offer ("we book the call, qualify the prospect, AND warm them up before handoff") makes it look like an operator. The shopping-cart shape rarely sells.

The Fazio "Done For You" pillar lives here — the more logistical intensity captured, the stronger DFY reads.

---

## Two products, same ICP

One product + one ICP = one cold-traffic offer. Two products with the same ICP each produce their own offer independently. Build one at a time.

If a single product targets multiple roles (CEO, marketing director), produce one offer per role — the New Money meaning differs.

---

## Evaluating an inherited offer

Before declaring an offer ready, run it through this check:

- **One sentence?** Can the offer be stated in one sentence a stranger would understand?
- **Specific claim?** Is the result quantified in a dimension the buyer cares about?
- **In paying-attention distance?** Is the number large enough to interest a buyer with budget?
- **Real urgency?** Is there a genuine constraint (deadline, capacity, price window)? (Optional but powerful.)
- **Evidence or guarantee or both?** Does the prospect have a reason to believe?
- **Differentiated on a real axis?** Less work, faster, less risk — pick one and prove it.

If any of these fail, flag it. The offer needs reframing, not copy.
